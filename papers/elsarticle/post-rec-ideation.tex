\begin{abstract}
The growth of scientific literature has produced a situation in which researchers can access a large amount of papers but cannot easily transform this access into a reliable understanding of what is already known, what remains open, and which research ideas are actually promising. This problem, referred to in this paper as \emph{literature overload}, is especially relevant in fast-moving areas such as recommender systems, retrieval-augmented generation, large language model agents, and scientific discovery. This article presents \textsc{POST-Rec} (Paper-Oriented Scientific Topic Recommender), a literature-grounded recommender system for research ideation. The proposed method treats research ideas as recommendation items and uses bibliographic evidence as the main source for modeling their relevance, novelty, and feasibility. The system integrates dual-pass retrieval over OpenAlex, deterministic relevance filtering, hybrid lexical and neural re-ranking, explicit state-of-the-art tiering, landscape construction, gap-matrix modeling, proposal generation, critic-based validation, and a facet-level extension called Facet-Grounded Gap Verification (FGGV). The model incorporates a multifaceted analysis of papers, including recency, citation strength, topical relevance, evidence coverage, gap alignment, and contrastive facet novelty. In this way, \textsc{POST-Rec} aims to recommend research directions that are not only aligned with the user's interests but also grounded in the current literature and supported by inspectable artifacts.
\end{abstract}

\begin{highlights}
\item Literature-grounded research ideation is modeled as a recommender system problem.
\item Explicit state-of-the-art and foundation tiers are used to control the evidence context.
\item Landscape summaries and gap matrices are generated before proposal construction.
\item FGGV verifies novelty and gap alignment at problem, method, data, and evaluation facets.
\item The proposal follows an inspectable structure inspired by social-capital recommender studies.
\end{highlights}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Scientific communication has expanded rapidly in recent years, creating a complex environment in which researchers need to read, compare, and interpret a large set of papers before deciding which direction should be investigated. Online scholarly indexes make this literature accessible, but access alone does not guarantee understanding. A researcher who wants to propose a new contribution in retrieval-augmented generation, recommender systems, or scientific discovery may need to analyze hundreds or thousands of papers, identify the most recent advances, distinguish seminal works from outdated ones, and understand which combinations of problem, method, data, and evaluation remain underserved. In this scenario, the researcher can be exposed to a condition similar to information overload in online social networks: the amount of available content is much greater than the individual's capacity to process it in a limited time.

Recommender systems (RSs) have emerged as an important solution for overload problems, because they filter items according to a model of user interests and contextual evidence~\cite{adomavicius2005toward,ricci2015handbook}. In social media, the items are posts, news, videos, users, or other resources. In scientific ideation, however, the items can be research directions, open gaps, paper topics, methodological combinations, or structured proposals. The challenge is therefore not only to retrieve papers, but also to transform them into recommendations that are relevant, novel, feasible, and grounded in evidence. This transformation requires more than a generic language model prompt, because the model must know which papers were considered, why they were selected, and how the idea differs from them.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) provides a useful foundation for this type of system by grounding language model output in retrieved documents~\cite{lewis2020rag}. Nevertheless, RAG alone does not define what counts as state of the art, does not guarantee that recent papers are present in the context, and does not require an explicit gap matrix before the idea is generated. Recent systems for scientific ideation, such as ResearchAgent~\cite{baek2025researchagent}, NOVA~\cite{hu2025nova}, SciMON~\cite{wang2024scimon}, Scideator~\cite{roy2024scideator}, Chain of Ideas~\cite{li2025coi}, and the Idea Novelty Checker~\cite{ramesh2025inc}, demonstrate that language models can support literature exploration and novelty assessment. However, the artifacts that connect papers to ideas are often implicit, hidden in prompts, or not structured in a way that can be audited by the user.

This paper presents \textsc{POST-Rec}, a Paper-Oriented Scientific Topic Recommender that investigates, models, and quantifies literature-grounded research ideation. The proposed approach is inspired by recommender systems that exploit social context, influence, interactions, and item features to improve personalization. In our setting, the system exploits \emph{evidence capital}: the accumulated value of bibliographic resources, citation signals, methodological facets, and explicit gaps that can be extracted from the scientific literature. Instead of recommending content only because it is textually similar to a seed topic, the system recommends research ideas according to their relationship with current papers, foundation papers, gap cells, and facet-level differences.

The intuition behind this work is that a research idea is more valuable when it is grounded in a relevant body of literature, connected to a clear open gap, different from the most similar papers, and feasible enough to be developed as a paper or project. Therefore, a recommender system for research ideation should not only rank candidate ideas by semantic similarity. It should also model the strength of evidence, the recency of the supporting literature, the saturation of similar approaches, and the diversity of the final recommendation list. In this case, the value of a recommended idea is associated with the evidence structure behind it, beyond traditional similarity metrics of interest.

The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The evidence-capital recommendation model}, which represents research ideas as items ranked by relevance, evidence strength, SOTA alignment, novelty, and gap alignment;
  \item \textbf{The user expectation model}, which comprises seed topics, research area, constraints, depth, and feedback signals used to build and update the user's ideation profile;
  \item \textbf{The SOTA-grounded retrieval pipeline}, which uses dual-pass OpenAlex retrieval, deterministic filtering, hybrid re-ranking, and explicit quotas for recent and foundation papers;
  \item \textbf{The Facet-Grounded Gap Verification model}, which analyzes problem, method, data, and evaluation facets to verify whether an idea is aligned with underserved gaps and different from existing literature;
  \item \textbf{The proposal artifact structure}, which materializes landscape summaries, gap matrices, proposal JSON objects, critic reports, and ranking components for inspection.
\end{itemize}

The remaining aspects of this paper are structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} provides background on recommender systems and literature-grounded ideation. Section~\ref{sec:related} discusses related works. Section~\ref{sec:proposal} introduces the proposed recommendation model, including the overview, notation, data modeling, metrics, algorithms, and motivation scenario. After the proposal, only the bibliographic references are presented.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

This section provides an overview of two important topics for the proposed method: recommender systems and literature-grounded research ideation. The objective is not to present an exhaustive survey, but to describe the concepts used later to model \textsc{POST-Rec} as a recommendation pipeline.

\subsection{Recommender systems}

Recommender systems are computational techniques designed to suggest relevant items to users based on preferences, behavior, context, or item characteristics~\cite{ricci2015handbook}. Traditional approaches include collaborative filtering, content-based filtering, and hybrid methods that combine multiple signals~\cite{adomavicius2005toward}. In content-based filtering, items are recommended because their features are similar to items previously preferred by the user. In collaborative filtering, recommendations are inferred from similarities among users or from interaction matrices. Hybrid models combine these perspectives to overcome limitations such as data sparsity, cold start, and over-specialization.

In the context of scientific literature, the user is usually a researcher and the item can be a paper, an author, a venue, a topic, or a research gap. Several signals can be used to model relevance, such as the overlap between a paper abstract and the user's query, citation count, publication year, venue quality, semantic embedding similarity, or citation-graph proximity. Nevertheless, recommending \emph{ideas} is more complex than recommending papers. A paper can be retrieved and shown directly. An idea, on the other hand, must be synthesized from multiple papers and should explain how it uses, contrasts, or extends prior work.

Thus, the proposed system adopts the recommender-system view but extends the item representation. The item is not a raw paper; it is a structured proposal with fields such as title, problem, motivation, method, expected contribution, evidence papers, novelty delta, risks, and possible evaluation plan. This representation allows the system to compute ranking features that are not available for raw documents, including gap alignment and facet novelty.

\subsection{Literature overload and research ideation}

The term literature overload denotes the difficulty of processing the growing volume of scientific publications in a specific research area. The problem is particularly relevant in areas where preprints, conference papers, and software artifacts appear quickly. A researcher may find many works that are partially related to the same idea, but only a smaller subset corresponds to the most recent state of the art. In addition, seminal works may be highly cited but not necessarily representative of the latest methods. Therefore, a literature-grounded ideation system needs to balance recent and foundation papers.

Large language models can support this process by summarizing papers, comparing concepts, and generating candidate ideas. However, unconstrained generation can produce generic proposals, invented references, or ideas that are already saturated in the literature~\cite{lu2024aiscientist,si2025llmideas}. A reliable system should therefore separate retrieval from generation, enforce evidence-only constraints, and create intermediate artifacts that can be reviewed by the user.

\subsection{Evidence capital}

In social-capital recommender systems, the value of an item can be estimated through interactions, influence, trust, reputation, and network relationships. In \textsc{POST-Rec}, we define an analogous notion called \emph{Evidence Capital}. Evidence Capital is the accumulated value that a research idea receives from the papers, facets, gaps, and validation signals that support it. A candidate idea has high evidence capital when it is connected to recent and relevant papers, addresses an underserved gap, is distinct from closely related works, and has a coherent evaluation path.

The concept does not replace novelty or relevance. Instead, it organizes them into a single recommender-system perspective. Relevance indicates whether the idea fits the user's topic. Novelty indicates whether it differs from the retrieved literature. Evidence strength indicates whether it is supported by trustworthy papers. Gap alignment indicates whether it targets an underserved cell in the literature landscape. Facet diversity indicates whether the final list of ideas avoids redundant variants of the same problem or method.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

In the realm of literature-grounded ideation, several methodologies have been explored, each with a particular strategy for generating or verifying research ideas. Some approaches emphasize retrieval and summarization, others focus on automated scientific discovery, while recent systems introduce novelty checking and human-in-the-loop evaluation. These works are closely related to the proposed method, but they differ in how they model gaps, state-of-the-art evidence, and facet-level novelty.

Si et al.~\cite{si2025llmideas} investigate whether large language models can generate novel research ideas and evaluate them with a large-scale human study. This work is important because it treats scientific ideation as an empirical object of study, rather than assuming that generated ideas are useful. However, the generation process does not expose a structured gap matrix or a reusable facet map. In contrast, \textsc{POST-Rec} materializes these artifacts before proposing ideas, so that the relationship between literature evidence and final suggestions is visible.

ResearchAgent~\cite{baek2025researchagent} proposes an iterative research idea generation process over scientific literature. Its main strength is the repeated exploration of papers and refinement of ideas. NOVA~\cite{hu2025nova} also explores retrieval feedback to improve model output. These systems indicate that iterative retrieval and planning can improve scientific generation, but their focus is not necessarily on defining SOTA tiers or producing a transparent gap matrix. \textsc{POST-Rec} adopts a more constrained pipeline: the system first retrieves recent and foundation papers, then filters and ranks them, then builds the landscape, and only after that generates proposals.

SciMON~\cite{wang2024scimon} is relevant because it optimizes scientific inspiration for novelty. Chain of Ideas~\cite{li2025coi} models temporal chains in the evolution of literature. These works are useful for representing how research ideas evolve over time. Nevertheless, the novelty unit is typically a whole idea or a chain, while the proposed FGGV extension checks novelty at the level of problem, method, data, and evaluation facets. This distinction is important because an idea may appear novel as a whole but still repeat a saturated method or dataset.

Scideator~\cite{roy2024scideator} and the Idea Novelty Checker~\cite{ramesh2025inc} are especially close to this work because they address facet-based generation or verification. Their focus shows that scientific novelty can be decomposed into smaller units. However, facet-level analysis is frequently used after idea generation or as an interface for user exploration. In \textsc{POST-Rec}, facets are integrated into the recommendation score. The Literature Facet Map (LFM) is built from the retrieved papers, and the Gap-Facet Alignment (GFA) score is computed by comparing proposal facets against gap-matrix cells.

RankGPT-style re-ranking~\cite{sun2023rankgpt} and hybrid retrieval methods~\cite{zuccon2015integrating} motivate the use of lexical and neural signals during paper ranking. OpenAlex~\cite{priem2022openalex} provides the open scholarly index used for retrieval. These components are not sufficient by themselves to generate research proposals, but they are necessary to provide a reproducible and inspectable evidence context.

\section{The proposal}
\label{sec:proposal}

This section outlines the proposed \textsc{POST-Rec} system. Firstly, the recommendation overview is presented. After that, the bibliographic data features and notation are described. Following this, the discussion moves to data modeling, state-of-the-art definition, evidence-capital metrics, Facet-Grounded Gap Verification, and recommendation algorithms. Finally, a motivation scenario is presented to demonstrate how the proposed model can be applied in practice.

\subsection{Recommendation overview}

The proposed recommender system aims to generate research ideas based on scientific literature data. It uses a variety of metrics to assess the evidence capital of papers and candidate proposals, including topical relevance, publication recency, citation strength, SOTA alignment, gap alignment, facet novelty, and final-list diversity. These scores are calculated from bibliographic features, paper abstracts, user expectations, and structured artifacts produced during the pipeline. By assessing these factors, the system ranks ideas that are likely to be more relevant and more useful for a researcher.

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} presents an overview of the recommendation system proposed by this paper. The architecture is divided into five main steps.

    \textbf{Server Side:} API gateway, OpenAlex crawler, paper database, vector database\\[2mm]
    \textbf{Engine:} Feature engineering $\rightarrow$ SOTA re-ranking $\rightarrow$ landscape and gap matrix $\rightarrow$ proposal generation $\rightarrow$ FGGV ranking

  \caption{Recommendation overview, which shows the macro steps to generate literature-grounded research ideas.}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Web application.} This step comprises the interface in which the researcher describes the topic, research area, constraints, and desired depth. It also collects feedback after recommendations are generated.
  \item \textbf{Bibliographic retrieval service.} This service connects the application to OpenAlex and retrieves recent and foundation papers related to the user's expectation.
  \item \textbf{Paper processing.} This background service normalizes metadata, removes duplicates, filters irrelevant papers, and extracts features from titles, abstracts, years, citation counts, and topic fields.
  \item \textbf{Recommendation model.} In this step, all recommendation metrics are calculated. The system builds a SOTA landscape, constructs a gap matrix, generates proposals, and verifies their evidence links.
  \item \textbf{Research idea service.} This service returns the ranked list of ideas, including evidence papers, novelty explanations, gap alignments, and warnings produced by the critic.
\end{enumerate}

This organization follows the principle that recommendation quality depends on modeling both the user and the item. In \textsc{POST-Rec}, the user is modeled by the expectation and feedback profile, while the item is modeled by a structured research proposal and its supporting evidence.

\subsection{Motivation scenario}
\label{subsec:scenario}

This section showcases the application of the proposed recommender system. The idea is to present a practical example of the algorithm's utility in analyzing and evaluating evidence-capital metrics for two simulated research ideas. The analysis walks through the entire recommendation process, including retrieval, relevance, SOTA anchors, gap alignment, facet novelty, and ranking.

Consider a researcher whose expectation is: ``I want to propose a new work on retrieval-augmented generation for scientific ideation in recommender systems''. The system expands this expectation into terms such as \emph{research ideation}, \emph{scientific discovery}, \emph{RAG}, \emph{LLM agents}, \emph{facet novelty}, and \emph{recommender systems}. After dual-pass retrieval and filtering, the system builds a landscape with papers on RAG, ideation agents, novelty checking, and hybrid retrieval.

Two candidate ideas are generated:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Idea A:} A RAG-based assistant that summarizes papers and generates research ideas from the top retrieved documents.
  \item \textbf{Idea B:} A recommender system that builds a gap matrix from SOTA and foundation papers, extracts problem-method-data-evaluation facets, and ranks ideas by contrastive facet novelty and gap alignment.
\end{itemize}

Idea A has high relevance because it matches the user's topic and uses current RAG papers. However, the facet map indicates that several retrieved works already use paper summarization and direct idea generation. Its method facet is therefore saturated. Idea B has similar topical relevance but adds an explicit gap matrix and facet-grounded novelty verification. In this case, its method and evaluation facets have lower saturation and higher alignment with underserved gap cells. Thus, Idea B receives a higher evidence-capital score and is ranked above Idea A.

This example illustrates why semantic similarity alone is not sufficient. Both ideas are related to the user's expectation, but the second idea has more explicit novelty and a clearer connection to open gaps. The proposed metric therefore aims to recommend ideas whose value is supported by the structure of the literature, not only by textual overlap.

\subsection{Bibliographic data features}

This paper uses OpenAlex for retrieval purposes. However, the recommendation model may be implemented with other scholarly indexes, such as Semantic Scholar, Crossref, arXiv, or domain-specific repositories, as long as they provide the basic concepts required by the model. Each retrieved paper is treated as a bibliographic item that may contain a title, abstract, DOI, publication year, venue, citation count, author list, concepts, and source identifier.

The following features are used by the current model:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Title and abstract.} These fields are used to compute lexical overlap, BM25 ranking, dense embeddings, and facet extraction.
  \item \textbf{Publication year.} This field is used to define the SOTA tier and to compute recency.
  \item \textbf{Citation count.} This field is used to identify foundation papers and to compute a bounded citation-strength signal.
  \item \textbf{Concepts and fields.} These metadata help filter papers that are outside the user's research area.
  \item \textbf{Identifiers.} DOI and OpenAlex identifiers are used for deduplication and evidence validation.
\end{itemize}

OpenAlex provides an API that enables the system to access works, authors, venues, concepts, citations, and related metadata~\cite{priem2022openalex}. In this work, the retrieval process is intentionally simple and reproducible. The system queries the index twice: one pass for recent papers and one pass for highly cited foundation papers. This avoids the situation in which a single ranking list is dominated only by recent low-impact papers or by older highly cited works.

A user expectation is represented as
\begin{equation}
  E = \langle T_{seed}, A, O, D, C \rangle,
\end{equation}
where $O$ is the objective of the run, $D$ is the desired depth, and $C$ is a set of optional constraints. The expanded topic set is defined by
\begin{equation}
  T^* = \mathrm{unique}(T_{seed} \cup T_{learned} \cup T_{techniques}),
  \label{eq:topic-expansion}
\end{equation}
where $T_{techniques}$ contains technical terms extracted from previously approved ideas or from the selected research area. The query token set is $Q = \mathrm{tokens}(T^* \cup A)$ after stopword removal and token filtering.

Papers are represented as
\begin{equation}
  p = \langle title, abstract, year, citations, venue, concepts, doi, e_p \rangle,
\end{equation}
where $e_p$ is the embedding vector of the paper text. Candidate ideas are represented as
\begin{equation}
  i = \langle title, problem, method, data, evaluation, evidence, gaps, risks, e_i \rangle.
\end{equation}
The tuple representation allows the system to score ideas using both bibliographic and proposal-level features.

\subsection{Data modeling and preprocessing}

All extracted papers are preprocessed and modeled before generation. As a result of preprocessing, the paper model contains textual features, bibliographic metadata, and vector representations. The objective of this step is to reduce noise and create a consistent input for ranking and facet extraction.

\subsubsection{Paper preprocessing}

The preprocessing pipeline performs the following steps:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cleaning.} Titles and abstracts are normalized to lowercase, while punctuation, duplicated spaces, and malformed markup are removed.
  \item \textbf{Tokenization.} The text is broken into tokens, allowing the system to compute overlap between papers and the user's query.
  \item \textbf{Stopword removal.} Common words that add little meaning are removed, emphasizing significant terms.
  \item \textbf{Deduplication.} Papers are deduplicated using DOI, OpenAlex identifier, normalized title, and year.
  \item \textbf{Embedding generation.} Each paper receives a dense vector representation used for semantic retrieval and novelty comparison.
  \item \textbf{Facet extraction.} The system extracts problem, method, data, and evaluation statements from each retained paper.
\end{enumerate}

The relevance of a paper is initially calculated through lexical overlap. Let $X_p$ be the token set obtained from the title and abstract of paper $p$. The overlap between $X_p$ and query tokens $Q$ is
\begin{equation}
  O(X_p,Q) = \frac{|X_p \cap Q|}{|Q|}, \quad Q \neq \emptyset.
\end{equation}
The final paper relevance score is defined by
\begin{align}
  s_{rel}(p) &= w_tO(T_{title}(p),Q) + w_bO(T_{body}(p),Q) + b_{cite}(p) - p_{avoid}, \label{eq:paper-rel}\\
  b_{cite}(p) &= \min\left(\frac{\ln(1+c_p)}{\delta}, b_{max}\right), \label{eq:cite-boost}
\end{align}
where $T_{title}$ denotes title tokens, $T_{body}$ denotes title plus abstract tokens, $b_{cite}$ is a bounded citation boost, and $p_{avoid}$ is a penalty applied when the paper matches topics previously rejected by the user. In the current implementation, the default values are $w_t=0.55$, $w_b=0.30$, $\delta=12$, and $b_{max}=0.15$.

A paper is retained if
\begin{equation}
  p \in \mathcal{P} \Leftrightarrow s_{rel}(p) \geq \tau_{rel},
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{rel}=0.22$ by default. If both title and abstract overlap are very low, the score is capped before applying the final threshold. This prevents highly cited but off-topic papers from entering the generation context.

\subsubsection{User profile modeling}

The user's profile is built from explicit expectations and implicit feedback. At the beginning of a run, the profile is defined by seed topics, research area, depth, and constraints. After the system produces recommendations, the user may rate ideas. Positive ratings update $T_{learned}$, while negative ratings update avoided topics. The profile can therefore change over time, just as a social-network recommendation profile changes when the user interacts with posts.

This dynamic modeling is important because research interests are not fixed. A user may start with a broad topic such as ``scientific ideation with LLMs'' and later prefer narrower directions such as ``facet-level novelty'', ``gap matrices'', or ``SOTA verification''. In this case, the recommender system should increase the probability of retrieving papers and generating ideas related to these learned topics.

\subsection{State-of-the-art tier definition}

A central design choice in \textsc{POST-Rec} is to define state of the art explicitly. The system uses two complementary tiers: recent SOTA papers and foundation papers. A paper belongs to the recent tier if
\begin{equation}
  p \in \mathcal{S}_{sota} \Leftrightarrow y_p \geq y_{now} - Y,
\end{equation}
where $Y$ is the recency window, set to three years by default. A paper belongs to the foundation tier if
\begin{equation}
  p \in \mathcal{S}_{found} \Leftrightarrow c_p \geq \tau_c,
\end{equation}
where $\tau_c$ is the citation threshold, set to 50 by default.

The reason for using two tiers is that recent papers and foundation papers play different roles. Recent papers are useful to identify current methods, new datasets, and open limitations. Foundation papers are useful to understand established terminology, theoretical background, and methodological roots. A good idea should usually be aware of both groups, but it should not be grounded only in old literature when the topic is evolving quickly.

After retrieval and ranking, a quota $\phi$ is applied to the context window:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{|\mathcal{P}_k \cap \mathcal{S}_{sota}|}{|\mathcal{P}_k|} \geq \phi.
\end{equation}
The default value is $\phi=0.6$, meaning that at least 60\% of the top context papers should be recent SOTA papers whenever enough relevant recent papers exist. This rule makes SOTA selection explicit and reproducible.

\subsection{Hybrid re-ranking}

Each retained paper receives a sparse score and a dense score. The sparse score is based on BM25 and captures exact lexical matching. The dense score is based on cosine similarity between the query embedding and the paper embedding. The hybrid score is
\begin{equation}
  H(p,Q) = \lambda s_{BM25}(p,Q) + (1-\lambda) \cos(e_p,e_Q),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ controls the contribution of lexical relevance. In the current implementation, $\lambda=0.4$. The system also uses vector database retrieval to ensure that semantically similar papers are not lost when exact words differ.

The intuition behind this hybrid model is that scientific language contains both stable technical terms and diverse descriptions of similar concepts. BM25 captures exact terms such as ``retrieval-augmented generation'' or ``facet novelty'', while dense embeddings capture related expressions such as ``evidence-grounded generation'' or ``novelty verification''. Combining both signals reduces the risk of missing relevant papers due to vocabulary variation.

\subsection{Evidence Capital metric}
\label{subsec:ecmetric}

The Evidence Capital metric aims to measure the value of a candidate research idea based on the amount and quality of evidence that supports it. It is inspired by the idea that a recommendation item can be more relevant when it has more meaningful interactions and stronger context. In this work, the interactions are bibliographic and conceptual rather than social. They include the relationship between the idea and recent papers, foundation papers, gap cells, and facets.

For a candidate idea $i$, the Evidence Capital score is computed as
\begin{equation}
  EC(i) = \alpha R(i) + \beta S(i) + \gamma N(i) + \sigma G(i) + \epsilon F(i) + \zeta D(i),
  \label{eq:ec}
\end{equation}
where $R(i)$ is relevance to the user expectation, $S(i)$ is SOTA support, $N(i)$ is novelty, $G(i)$ is gap alignment, $F(i)$ is facet novelty, and $D(i)$ is diversity contribution in the final list. The parameters $\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\sigma,\epsilon,\zeta$ are weights allocated to each component and cumulatively sum to 1.

\subsubsection{Relevance score}

The relevance of an idea measures how well it matches the user's expectation. It combines lexical overlap between idea text and query terms with dense similarity between the idea embedding and the expectation embedding:
\begin{equation}
  R(i) = \rho O(T_i,Q) + (1-\rho)\cos(e_i,e_E),
\end{equation}
where $T_i$ is the token set of idea $i$, $e_i$ is the idea embedding, $e_E$ is the expectation embedding, and $\rho$ controls lexical contribution.

\subsubsection{SOTA support score}

SOTA support evaluates whether an idea is anchored in recent evidence. Let $A_i$ be the set of evidence papers linked to idea $i$. The SOTA support is
\begin{equation}
  S(i) = \frac{|A_i \cap \mathcal{S}_{sota}|}{|A_i|}, \quad A_i \neq \emptyset.
\end{equation}
Ideas without evidence anchors receive $S(i)=0$ and are marked by the critic as invalid. The goal is to ensure that every idea has at least one recent paper supporting its motivation or differentiation.

\subsubsection{Novelty score}

Novelty is measured by comparing the candidate idea against retrieved papers and previously generated ideas. A simple document-level novelty score is
\begin{equation}
  N(i) = 1 - \max_{p \in \mathcal{P}_k}\cos(e_i,e_p).
\end{equation}
A low value indicates that the idea is close to an existing paper and may represent a rephrasing. A high value indicates larger semantic distance, but this does not necessarily mean the idea is good. Therefore, novelty is combined with relevance and evidence strength in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ec}.

\subsubsection{Gap alignment score}

The system constructs a gap matrix $G$ from the retrieved literature. Rows represent problem families and columns represent method, data, or evaluation families. Each cell is labeled as well-covered, partial, or underserved. The gap alignment score is
\begin{equation}
  G(i) = \frac{1}{|C_i|}\sum_{c \in C_i} g(c),
\end{equation}
where $C_i$ is the set of gap cells addressed by the idea and $g(c)$ maps underserved cells to higher values. This score rewards proposals that target parts of the landscape that appear less explored.

\subsubsection{Facet novelty score}

Facet novelty verifies whether the problem, method, data, and evaluation facets of an idea differ from the most similar facets in the literature. Let $f \in \{problem, method, data, evaluation\}$ and $F_f(i)$ be the facet text of idea $i$. The facet novelty index is
\begin{equation}
  FNI_f(i) = 1 - \max_{p \in \mathcal{P}_k}\cos(e_{F_f(i)},e_{F_f(p)}).
\end{equation}
The final facet novelty score is
\begin{equation}
  F(i) = \sum_f \omega_f FNI_f(i),
\end{equation}
where $\omega_f$ controls the importance of each facet. In exploratory domains, method novelty may be emphasized. In empirical domains, data and evaluation novelty may receive higher weights.

\subsubsection{Diversity contribution}

The diversity score reduces redundancy in the final recommendation list. Let $L$ be the list of already selected ideas. The diversity contribution of a new idea $i$ is
\begin{equation}
  D(i) = 1 - \max_{j \in L}\cos(e_i,e_j).
\end{equation}
This step prevents the system from recommending several small variations of the same idea. It also helps the user explore different directions within the same research area.

\subsection{Facet-Grounded Gap Verification}
\label{subsec:fggv}

Facet-Grounded Gap Verification (FGGV) is an extension of the SOTA pipeline. It was designed to address a limitation of document-level novelty checks: a proposal can look different at the surface while still using the same problem, method, dataset, or evaluation setup already saturated in the literature. FGGV therefore decomposes papers and proposals into facets and compares them against a structured literature map.

\subsubsection{Literature Facet Map}

For each paper in $\mathcal{P}_k$, the system extracts four facets: problem, method, data, and evaluation. These facets are stored in a Literature Facet Map (LFM). Each entry includes the facet text, embedding vector, paper identifier, and saturation score. Saturation measures how common a facet is in the current retrieved landscape. A method facet that appears in many papers has high saturation, while a rare combination of data and evaluation has lower saturation.

\subsubsection{Gap-Facet Alignment}

Each candidate idea must declare which gap cells it addresses and how each facet contributes to those cells. The Gap-Facet Alignment score measures this consistency. If an idea claims to address an underserved evaluation gap but its evaluation facet is generic or unrelated, the GFA score is low. If the problem, method, data, and evaluation facets all point to the same underserved cell, the GFA score is high.

\subsubsection{Contrastive facet novelty}

For each proposal facet, FGGV retrieves the nearest facets from the LFM and asks whether the proposal facet is a repetition, a small variation, or a meaningful contrast. This contrastive step is important because embedding distance alone can be misleading. Two methods may use different words while implementing the same strategy, or similar words while describing different assumptions. The contrastive comparison provides a more nuanced signal for novelty.

\subsubsection{Facet diversity selection}

After computing EC and FGGV scores, the final list is selected with a diversity-aware procedure. The system prefers ideas that have high evidence capital and also contribute different facet profiles to the final set. This means that a slightly lower-scoring idea can be selected if it covers a different problem or method direction and increases the diversity of the list.

The FGGV score is defined as
\begin{equation}
  FGGV(i) = \eta EC(i) + \mu GFA(i) + \nu F(i) + \kappa D(i),
\end{equation}
where $\eta,\mu,\nu,\kappa$ are configurable weights. This score is used to rank proposals in FGGV mode.

\subsection{Recommendation model}

The recommendation model capitalizes on evidence-capital scores to curate personalized research ideas for users. The process starts with a set of retrieved papers, followed by feature engineering and state-of-the-art tiering. Then the system constructs a landscape and a gap matrix. Candidate proposals are generated from the underserved cells and verified by a critic. Finally, the ideas are ranked by EC or FGGV, depending on the selected mode.

The model supports four generation modes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quick mode:} generates ideas from the top-ranked papers without constructing a full landscape or gap matrix;
  \item \textbf{SOTA mode:} constructs the landscape and gap matrix before generating ideas;
  \item \textbf{Exploratory mode:} uses the same structure as SOTA but increases the weight of novelty and diversity;
  \item \textbf{FGGV mode:} extends SOTA mode with facet maps, gap-facet alignment, contrastive facet novelty, and diversity-aware selection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Recommendation algorithms}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:paper-retrieval} describes the paper retrieval and ranking process. The input is a user expectation, and the output is a ranked evidence set.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Paper retrieval and SOTA ranking}
\label{alg:paper-retrieval}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require User expectation $E$
\Ensure Ranked paper set $\mathcal{P}_k$
\State $T^* \gets \mathrm{expandTopics}(T_{seed}, T_{learned}, A)$
\State $Q \gets \mathrm{tokens}(T^* \cup A)$
\State $\mathcal{D}_{recent} \gets \mathrm{OpenAlexSearch}(Q, year \geq y_{now}-Y)$
\State $\mathcal{D}_{found} \gets \mathrm{OpenAlexSearch}(Q, citations \geq \tau_c)$
\State $\mathcal{D} \gets \mathrm{deduplicate}(\mathcal{D}_{recent} \cup \mathcal{D}_{found})$
\ForAll{$p \in \mathcal{D}$}
  \State $s_{rel}(p) \gets \mathrm{paperRelevance}(p,Q)$
  \If{$s_{rel}(p) < \tau_{rel}$}
    \State remove $p$ from $\mathcal{D}$
  \EndIf
  \State $H(p,Q) \gets \lambda s_{BM25}(p,Q) + (1-\lambda)\cos(e_p,e_Q)$
\EndFor
\State $\mathcal{P}_k \gets \mathrm{rankWithSOTAQuota}(\mathcal{D},H,\phi)$
\State \Return $\mathcal{P}_k$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:idea-generation} describes the recommendation model with FGGV. Lines 1--3 construct the landscape and gap matrix. Lines 4--7 generate and validate proposals. Lines 8--13 compute EC and FGGV scores. Finally, the selected ideas are returned to the user.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Research idea recommendation with FGGV}
\label{alg:idea-generation}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require User expectation $E$, ranked papers $\mathcal{P}_k$
\Ensure Ranked idea list $L$
\State $Landscape \gets \mathrm{synthesizeLandscape}(\mathcal{P}_k)$
\State $G \gets \mathrm{buildGapMatrix}(Landscape)$
\State $LFM \gets \mathrm{buildLiteratureFacetMap}(\mathcal{P}_k)$
\State $\mathcal{I} \gets \mathrm{generateIdeas}(E,\mathcal{P}_k,G)$
\ForAll{$i \in \mathcal{I}$}
  \State $valid(i) \gets \mathrm{criticCheck}(i,\mathcal{P}_k,G)$
  \If{$valid(i)=false$}
    \State mark $i$ as needs refinement
  \EndIf
  \State $EC(i) \gets \mathrm{evidenceCapital}(i)$
  \State $GFA(i) \gets \mathrm{gapFacetAlignment}(i,G,LFM)$
  \State $F(i) \gets \mathrm{facetNovelty}(i,LFM)$
  \State $FGGV(i) \gets \eta EC(i)+\mu GFA(i)+\nu F(i)+\kappa D(i)$
\EndFor
\State $L \gets \mathrm{diversityAwareSelection}(\mathcal{I},FGGV)$
\State \Return $L$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Generated artifacts and auditability}

A relevant aspect of the proposed system is that it does not return only a list of ideas. It also stores the artifacts that explain how the ideas were produced. These artifacts include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{retrieved\_papers}: the papers retrieved from OpenAlex after deduplication;
  \item \texttt{ranked\_papers}: the retained papers after relevance filtering and hybrid re-ranking;
  \item \texttt{sota\_landscape}: a structured summary of themes, methods, datasets, and limitations;
  \item \texttt{gap\_matrix}: the table of well-covered, partial, and underserved cells;
  \item \texttt{proposal\_candidates}: the generated ideas before critic validation;
  \item \texttt{critic\_report}: validation issues, citation errors, and refinement notes;
  \item \texttt{final\_ranking}: EC, FGGV, and diversity scores for the selected ideas.
\end{itemize}

This artifact structure is important for trust. If a user disagrees with an idea, he can inspect whether the problem came from retrieval, gap modeling, facet extraction, or ranking. In addition, these artifacts make the system more suitable for academic use, because reviewers and supervisors can examine the evidence path rather than only the final generated text.

\subsection{Discussion inside the proposal}

Although this version of the article focuses on the proposal and does not include an experimental evaluation section, the system was designed to support future empirical analysis. The component scores can be used in online and offline evaluations, and the artifacts can be used for ablation studies. For example, one can compare SOTA mode against FGGV mode to measure the effect of facet verification, or remove the SOTA quota to evaluate whether recent papers improve perceived idea usefulness.

We can point out that the current proposal does not claim that generated ideas are automatically publishable. Instead, it provides a controlled recommender pipeline that helps researchers identify directions that are better grounded in evidence. The human researcher remains responsible for judging theoretical contribution, feasibility, ethical issues, and final research design. In this sense, \textsc{POST-Rec} should be understood as an expert-assistance system for research ideation, not as a replacement for scientific judgment.
